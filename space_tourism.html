Space tourism is human space travel for recreational purposes.
There are several different types of space tourism, including orbital,
suborbital and lunar space tourism. It has been done by Roscosmos; the
Russian space agency, and by private spaceflight companies.

As the shuttle program expanded in the early 1980s, NASA began a Space
Flight Participant program to allow citizens without scientific or
governmental roles to fly. However, concrete efforts to develop space tourism
only started in the 21st century because NASA's programs were canceled.
This is due to the Challenger disaster which caused the death of the passengers,
including one civilian.

Unfortunately, space tourism has an impact on the environment.
According to a 2010 study published in Geophysical Research Letters,
1,000 suborbital launches of hybrid rockets from a single location
would release a total of 600 tonnes of black carbon into the
stratosphere. This unbalance would cause the temperature to
decrease by about 0.4 °C in the tropics and subtropics, whereas
temperature at the poles would increase by between 0.2 and 1 °C.

The first tourists to go to the International Space Station paid at least
20 million USD. On June 7, 2019, NASA announced that a trip to the International
Space Station is planned to be priced at 35,000 USD per day for one astronaut,
and an estimated 50 million USD for a roundtrip.
Currently, a suborbital spaceflight can cost from 250,000 USD to 30 million USD,
depending on the spaceflight company.
